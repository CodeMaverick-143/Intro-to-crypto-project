How Cryptocurrency Works...?

Cryptocurrency operates on a decentralized network known as a blockchain. This technology underpins the functioning of various cryptocurrencies, including Bitcoin, Ethereum, and many others.

Blockchain Technology

At its core, a blockchain is a distributed ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers (nodes). Here's how it works:

  • Data Structure: Transactions are grouped into blocks, each containing a list of transactions, a timestamp, and a cryptographic hash of the previous block.
  • Immutability: Once added, data cannot be altered or deleted, ensuring integrity.
  • Decentralization: Blockchain is decentralized, maintained by nodes, and resistant to censorship.

Transaction Process

When a user initiates a transaction (e.g., sending cryptocurrency to another user), the following steps occur:

  • Broadcasting: The transaction is broadcast to the network and picked up by miners.
  • Validation: Miners validate the transaction to ensure the sender has sufficient balance and the transaction follows the rules.
Learn More

Mining

Mining is the process through which transactions are verified and added to the blockchain. Here's how it works:

  • Transaction Grouping: Validated transactions are grouped into a block.
  • Solving Mathematical Problems: Miners compete to solve puzzles (Proof of Work), and the first to solve it adds the block.
  • Reward: The miner who solves the puzzle gets rewarded with cryptocurrency.

Consensus Mechanisms

To ensure all nodes agree on the blockchain state, consensus mechanisms are employed. Some common ones include:

  • Proof of Work (PoW): Miners solve complex puzzles consuming computational power and energy.
  • Proof of Stake (PoS): Validators are chosen based on their staked coins, making it more energy-efficient.

Security Features

Cryptocurrencies use several security features:

  • Cryptographic Hashing: Blocks are linked with cryptographic hashes, making it almost impossible to alter data.
  • Public and Private Keys: Users have a pair of keys to ensure secure transactions—public (shared) and private (kept secret).

Wallets

Cryptocurrency wallets store, send, and receive cryptocurrencies. They can be:

  • Hot Wallets: Connected to the internet, convenient but more vulnerable to attacks.
  • Cold Wallets: Offline wallets (e.g., hardware wallets) providing better security.
